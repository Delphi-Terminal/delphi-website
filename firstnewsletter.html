Recession 

Recession risks have subsided in recent months, as key economic indicators point to a slowdown rather than an immediate downturn.

By Sahana Lydia

Mar 17, 2026

For recessionary risk, the yield curve has been argued to be one of the most reliable indicator to forecast an upcoming recession. The spread between the 10 year and 3 month treasury yields has moved back into positive territory, now around 0.62 percent, a sharp difference from previously being inverted.

This change removes one of the strongest recession signals. As long term rates are greater than short term rates, and 10-year treasury holding at around 4.22 percent shows the market is not expecting an immediate downturn.

However, the move back to a positive curve does not indicate strength. Instead, it shows a shift in expectations in which the markets are no longer anticipating an upcoming recession; they are simply adjusting for the continued pressure of growth at a slower and slower rate.

Volatility has also increased in recent days, with VIX moving to 23.5. This suggests that there is uncertainty in markets but that it’s not yet a crisis-level event. Noting that in previous crises, VIX has been well above 30.

In this regard, the credit markets are stable with high yield bonds trading at around 79.45 indicative of financial stress not being an issue currently. As with strong recessionary fear credit markets tend to weaken earlier as a result of default risk.

This is not a recession. It is a period of tightening conditions, and what follows depends on whether that pressure continues to build.

If interest rates remain high but volatility persists, financial conditions will tighten more, gradually slowing economic activity. But if credit stays stable and volatility eases, the economy can continue to expand without disruption.
