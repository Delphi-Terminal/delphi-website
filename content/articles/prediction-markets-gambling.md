As exchanges like Kalshi and Polymarket news start to dominate how people talk about crypto, elections, and the world, prediction markets are becoming more and more popular. But they’ve only come this far after a history of complex regulatory battles against the CFTC (Commodities Futures Trading Commission). Along with every other financial derivative governed by the CFTC, it has faced accusations of gambling. Mainstream narratives agree that it’s just gambling, not a tool for forecasting. Let’s debunk this.

**What mainstream narratives say**

Currently, [Axios](https://www.axios.com/2026/03/17/kalshi-polymarket-sports-betting) says that 61% of adults say prediction market trading is “more akin to gambling,” only 8% say “more akin to investing,” and also frames markets as a “controversial yet popular method for wagering” on sports, politics, business, news, and entertainment.

In addition, when [CNN](https://www.cnn.com/2026/03/21/politics/trump-prediction-markets-truth-predicts) was covering the Trump family’s Truth Predict platform, they repeatedly call prediction markets a “new facet of sports wagering” and says casino operators are alarmed by “sports‑style wagering” exploding on prediction markets, positioning the entire sector as an off‑casino betting product. Channels like CNN agree that prediction markets are far from forecasting tools or the next Bloomberg Terminal and closer to DraftKings. 

**Debunking Gambling Accusations** 

Look at the April Fed decision market sitting at $6.5M in volume on Kalshi and $27.2M on Polymarket. Right now, they’re both saying there’s about a 95% chance the Fed does nothing, a 3% chance of a rate hike, and tiny odds on any kind of cut. This is a live probability forecast that was sourced from the crowd on prediction markets of what the central bank will do, and this is the exact type of data that traders pay attention to, whether or not CNN called it a casino or not

Delphi Markets can further bring prediction markets closer to real signals in the macro world. Delphi Markets shows that the yes‑side, no‑side, and midprice all cluster around the mid‑50s over time in the price convergence panel, which is a sign that the market’s slowly agreeing on a fair price, not random swings. The microprice delta stays small and steady instead of spiking all over the place, which is what you expect when people are trading around a shared forecast. The Bid‑Ask Spread is flat and tight the entire time. In a casino/sports-betting, the house gives you a huge built‑in edge against you. Here, the gap between the best bid and best ask is just a few ticks. Most importantly, the market reacts to headlines and data, not to spur‑of‑the‑moment adrenaline like poker or sports-betting.

**My thoughts**

Sure, prediction markets still have a similar nature to gambling and sports-betting. But anyone who is against using prediction market data to make forecasts is missing out. The orderbook details, the depth in volume, the live probabilities, and more give traders real information to act on, and prices adjust differently that in sports-betting, contradicting what Axios and CNN reports. With tools like Delphi, the prediction market infrastructure layer, benchmarking elections, macro outcomes, and financial markets becomes easier and easier. 

I think the irony is that the same news outlets calling these platforms “gambling” will also quote their odds the moment they line up with a narrative about war, recession, or elections.
