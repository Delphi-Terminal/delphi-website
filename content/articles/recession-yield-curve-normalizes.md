For recessionary risk, the yield curve has been argued to be one of the most reliable indicators to forecast an upcoming recession. The spread between the 10 year and 3 month Treasury yields has moved back into positive territory, now around 0.62 percent, a sharp difference from previously being inverted.

This change removes one of the strongest recession signals. As long term rates are greater than short term rates, and the 10 year Treasury holding at around 4.22 percent shows the market is not expecting an immediate downturn.

A positive curve here is not a bullish signal, instead pointing to a shift in expectations, not a stronger economy.

## Volatility and Credit

Volatility has also increased in recent days, with the VIX moving to 23.5. This suggests that there is uncertainty in markets but that it is not yet a crisis level event. In previous crises, the VIX has been well above 30.

In this regard, the credit markets are stable with high yield bonds trading at around 79.45, indicative of financial stress not being an issue currently. As with strong recessionary fear, credit markets tend to weaken earlier as a result of default risk.

## What This Means

Looking less like recession and more like a tightening cycle. What follows depends on whether that pressure continues to build.

If interest rates remain high but volatility persists, financial conditions will tighten more, gradually slowing economic activity. But if credit stays stable and volatility eases, the economy can continue to expand without disruption.
